Introduction & Context

Disclaimer:

This entire lesson set was generated as an experiment using the /teach skill created by Matt Pocock (Learn Anything with my /teach skill). I have not reviewed the underlying generated information for mathematical or theoretical accuracy. Proceed with a healthy dose of skepticism!

This project started as a purely educational exploration of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) mechanics, but it quickly evolved into a fascinating meta-study on AI behavior and problem-solving.

Things became particularly interesting once I decided to put the theoretical MCTS concepts to the test by having the AI (Google's AntiGravity) build a Yahtzee-playing engine. Despite the AI's confident explanations and theoretical setup, the engine's actual performance was surprisingly poor—far below what the AI's confident dialogue had prepared me for.

This phenomenon jibes perfectly with my previous experiments trying to use Claude to write an expert-level checkers engine. In both cases, the AI confidently proposes a sophisticated architecture, is genuinely "surprised" when the empirical performance turns out to be mediocre, and then begins recursively suggesting "fixes" and "optimizations" that also fail to move the needle.

It serves as an incredible reminder of the "curse of dimensionality" in stochastic games, and the disconnect between an LLM's theoretical textbook knowledge and its ability to construct and debug complex, mathematically chaotic environments from scratch. Still, it was a highly entertaining and interesting experiment!
